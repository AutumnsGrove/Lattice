## What I Already Know

Patina runs nightly automated backups of every Grove database to cold storage.
Weekly archives compress the daily layers.
Twelve weeks of history remain quietly preserved.

It was called "Cache" before. That didn't fit.
Now it's "Patina."

Let me examine whether that's right.

---

## What IS a Patina?

In the physical world, a patina is:
- The green layer on copper and bronze
- Forms over TIME (years, decades)
- Not decay---PROTECTION
- The thing that saves the Statue of Liberty from corrosion
- Beautiful in its aged way
- Happens without anyone doing anything
- Silent. Invisible until you notice it.

```
    Raw copper       After years
         |               |
         v               v
     [bright]   -->   [green]
     [shiny]          [soft]
     [vulnerable]     [protected]
                      [beautiful]
```

The tagline we have: *"Age as armor. Time as protection."*

That's genuinely beautiful.

## The Concern: What About RECOVERY?

But here's my hesitation...

A patina is protective, yes. But you don't USE a patina.
You don't INTERACT with it. It just... is.

Backups are for RECOVERY.
Backups are INSURANCE.
Backups are the thing you REACH FOR when everything goes wrong.

```
    Normal day:                    Disaster day:

    You: *doesn't think           You: "Everything is gone!"
         about backups*                 |
         |                              v
         v                         +------------------+
    Patina: *silently              | PATINA, I        |
             accumulating*         | NEED YOU         |
                                   +------------------+
                                            |
                                            v

                                   [recovery complete]
                                   [tree lives again]
```

In that moment of crisis... does "Patina" feel like
something you can DEPEND ON?

Or does it feel too... passive?

## A New Direction: The Tree's Natural Protection

What does a tree do to protect itself?

When a tree is wounded:
- Sap flows to seal the wound
- Bark grows over
- The tree doesn't heal like animals
- It COMPARTMENTALIZES---walls off damage
- New growth covers old wounds

```
         Wound
           |
           v
        [damaged]
           |
           | time + natural process
           v
        [sealed]
        [protected]
        [bark grown over]
        [still there underneath]
        [recoverable if needed]
```

This is called "wound wood" or "callus tissue."

But those are ugly names.

## The Recovery Question, Revisited

Does "Patina" capture recovery?

Actually... maybe it doesn't have to.

The backup system isn't about recovery.
Recovery is what YOU do---contacting support, asking for a restore.

The backup system is about PROTECTION.
About the silent work that makes recovery possible.

Patina is the reason you CAN recover.
Patina isn't the recovery itself.

```
        Patina         You hit "contact support"       Recovery
        (the armor)    (asking for help)               (the restore)
            |                    |                          |
            v                    v                          v
        [silent]            [active]                   [result]
        [protective]        [human]                    [restored]
        [ongoing]           [one-time]                 [relief]
```

Patina isn't the restore button.
Patina is the reason there's something TO restore.

## The Verdict

**Patina is the right name.**

It doesn't capture "recovery" because recovery isn't the backup's job.
Recovery is the human act of asking for help (that's Porch).
Recovery is the restore process (that's operational).

Patina captures the ESSENCE:
- Silent
- Protective
- Time-accumulated
- Beautiful
- Unnoticed until you need it
- Already there

The concern about passivity? That IS the point.
You should never think about your backups.
You should never interact with them directly.
They should just... be there. Protecting.

Like the green on an old bronze bell.
Like the soft wear on a cherished book.
Like the marks of time that don't destroy---they preserve.
